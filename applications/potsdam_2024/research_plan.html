Outline

The ability to talk to each other in a peaceful and productive manner is paramount for a society drifting apart. The proposed research group tries to contribute in a small way to solving these issues by facilitating teaching methods that address competencies needed for an open discussion culture. For this, modern technologies and computer-enhanced methods are used to foster a learning environment that promotes tolerant and open speech.

Computer technology has become an indispensable tool in educational research and practice. However, there is a gap in the case of computer-assisted political education, specifically the support of deliberation with role-plays. The aim of the proposed research group is to fill in the blanks by developing a software framework that addresses these challenges. This environment will seamlessly integrate simulations, deliberation, and learning analytics in the political education classroom.

Technology plays a crucial role in enhancing political science education by supporting both teachers and students in various ways. For teachers, it lowers the bar for using advanced methods by facilitating content creation, enabling the use of simulations to build intuition [@calderon_systematic_2015;@connolly_systematic_2012;@vlachopoulos_effect_2017], tracking learning outcomes [@Dehne2018_compbase_mobile], and overcoming barriers related to distance, language, and culture. For students, technology creates immersive learning experiences [@dishon_making_2020], offers flexible options for distance and personalized learning, and supports constructivist learning approaches [@bada2015constructivism] by providing a foundation for individual opinion formation [@chang_study_2011;@lo_adolescents_2017] and enabling self-regulated, creative learning environments [@Paa_en_2022].

Political Education in Germany tries to build deliberative competencies such as taking on perspectives, discussing using arguments and moderating uncivil statements. Role-plays, a well-established method in political science education [@petrik2017handbuch;@duchatelet_unravelling_2020;@duchatelet_looking_2019], are particularly effective in fostering the ability to take on different perspectives, understand institutions and procedures. It enables the training of essential discussion, rhetoric, moderation, and academic leadership skills. Their complexity can vary: the simplest form is a discussion between two students taking pro and con sides of a current issue like reenacting an American presidential debate. But it can also be a more complex procedure like the Model United Nations [@duchatelet_explaining_2021] where students are selected in a tournament mode from all over the world to sit in the real United Nations venue and discuss world politics. Sometimes this kind of role-play is used as incubators for actual problems [@poole_becoming_2010]. Although simulations are not the only option to support political learning with technology, it is the most obvious choice. It is one of the strengths of computer technology to create a rich but unreal environment where social processes and political acts can be studied in a safe and ethical setting [@zender_potentials_2019]. The laboratory setting allows for repeatable administration so that results may be compared using different parameters and conditions, too.

The research group aims to develop innovative approaches to political education by integrating cutting-edge technologies, particularly artificial intelligence (AI), with established methods such as simulations and role-plays. The vision is to establish a network among Brandenburg universities focused on political education, computer science, and social media. The group’s mission is to bridge the gap between educational technologies and political education, creating interactive, practical learning experiences while enhancing the ability to analyze deliberative processes and discussions.

The core fields of action include three main areas: first, using AI to generate realistic up-to-date role-play scenarios that enhance learning outcomes; second, leveraging deliberation analytics to evaluate the quality of discussions in learning environments; and third, exploring how computer-assisted education can be enriched through these technologies.

Structurally, the group combines interdisciplinary expertise from computer science and political education, offering a rare and valuable blend of academic knowledge and hands-on engineering. This combination allows for both societal impact and scientific innovation. By addressing a key research gap, the group’s contribution lies in the development of a methodological toolkit that enhances political education through AI and deliberation analytics, pushing the boundaries of both fields. This initiative will lead to the creation of a collaborative network that strengthens interdisciplinary work and advances the study of political education in the digital age.

The proposed research group will be situated in the computer science faculty and focus on the construction and accompanying research of the learning environment. It is thus clearly located in the engineering field and will follow the customary methodology in that area [@peffers_design_2007;@vom_brocke_introduction_2020].

State of Research & Technology

Political education and similar pedagogies have been supported with various levels of computer technologies. These include websites [@asal_creating_2006;@chang_study_2011], emails [@handby_2021], PowerPoint [@meibauer_teaching_2018], mobile devices [@diacopoulos_systematic_2020], virtual games [@spangenberger_embodying_2024; @klaue2020] and 3d environments [@jamaludin_fostering_2009]. However, a targeted and comprehensive system is still missing that would support the whole process from the didactic example to the analytics of the learning outcomes.

In literature, there are several calls to action aimed at increasing the use of the role-play method and promoting the integration of computer-enhanced learning: @shellman_simulations_2006 argue that role-plays are an essential part of political science pedagogies and shift the focus from “saying” to “doing” which is important in developing skills alongside political knowledge. @kammerer_2021 title their paper “Simulations Research in Political Science Pedagogy: Where is Everyone?” They lament the heavy emphasis on publications and tenure, which leaves little room for exploring advanced pedagogical methods in political science. While role-playing techniques are commonplace in the field of international relations, they remain underutilized and insufficiently integrated into political science education in general. @kaiser2017einfachdigital argue that there is an initial hurdle because the technical construction of computer-supported role-plays would be complex, but if a system were in place, it could adapt well to changing asynchronous and distance-free scenarios. In summary, these calls to action highlight the method as highly relevant but hard to implement due to workload constraints. For this reason, computational methods are seen as an important enhancement, particularly in making the method more accessible and easier to integrate.

Simulations and role-plays are used in a number of application domains such as social work [@flaherty_teaching_2023], political theory [@handby_2021], climate change education [@fernandez_galeote_good_2022], business [@katzlinger_online_2008], in the medical field [@behler_eigentlich_2013] and many more. However, the method has the strongest match with the field of political education, as arguing, moderating, negotiating [@sun_assessing_2023] and taking on perspectives [@petrik2017alltagswelt] are prominent learning outcomes here. In the field of political education, the method is used mostly to teach about institutions and political systems [@cox_review_2014] that are complex and hard to connect to for the students such as the United Nations [@duchatelet_explaining_2021] or the European Union [@vermeiren_assessing_2022].

Although the preparation of the role-cards, scenario description, etc. are a lot of work, automated approaches have not reached the field of political education yet. Educators need to balance the overhead with the expected benefits when using the method. This effect is even stronger for serious games (educational computer games) [@malo_entwicklung_2008]. To address challenges in teachers’ workload, large language models such as ChatGPT have been used as a teacher assistant for lesson planning and personalization of learning material [@KASNECI2023102274], and generating personalized learning units [@kalota_primer_2024].

Serious games [@yi_designing_2011;@calderon_systematic_2015;@connolly_systematic_2012;@loh_serious_2015] have been a long time focus of the learning technologies community. However, there are few applications such as [@scheiner_2013] that try to integrate serious games and educational role-playing simulations. For one, the serious games movement came out of the rise of computer games; for another the role-playing is more related to a play (as in theater) than playing games. However, many questions about software architectures [@konert2013social], user experience [@moizer_approach_2019], learning analytics [@martinez_new_2022], and motivational arcs [@fernandez-sanchez_conceptual_2023] overlap between these modes. Therefore, it’s helpful to think of them as a continuum, depending on the extent to which characters are portrayed by humans or represented within a virtual environment.

In the extreme form the roles are reduced to a model of a human as a utility-maximizing rational actor [@avila-garzon_agent-based_2022;@andrade_sociological_2022]. This leads back to the political science research where rational choice experiments are used to model the effect of institutional changes or to understand the interplay of egoism and altruism in the human nature. As there already exists a software for the implementation of rational choice experiments [@chen_otreeopen-source_2016] that can be integrated.

To evaluate the outcome of political role-playing, a number of target learnings have been identified and studied. These include improved self-efficacy [@duchatelet_explaining_2021], negotiation skill [@sun_assessing_2023], structuring arguments [@sobernheim_structuring_2020] and the development of civic identities [@lo_adolescents_2017;@najjemba_engagement_2020]. In the learning technologies community tracking learning outcomes and persisting them consistently has been a focus of research [@Dehne2019_compbase_ple;@dehne2014].

All these learning outcomes combine to an improved deliberation level [@chambers_deliberative_2003]. The applicant has worked on creating software-packages i.e. [@dehne_dtrees_23;@dehne_social_media_24] to automatically analyze the quality of deliberation in discussions. He is also in the process of publishing a paper on the effect of moderation on deliberative quality using these measurements. These tools and insights can be transferred from the application field of analyzing deliberative quality in social media to the field of learning analytics. This is possible in the case of game-based learning as long as there is a live discussion as part of the game.

Supporting a learning process in its entirety has been part of the applicant’s research. @Dehne2021 presents a complete educational-technical model of inquiry-based learning from group-formation to learning analytics of peer-assessment. Moreover, it includes a ready-to-use implementation of the concepts, offering a level of complexity comparable to the intended support framework for political education.

The following section describes how the current state of research and the applicant’s previous experience in technological development can be developed further to support political education and discussion analytics.

Research Questions and Group Outline

The research group will tackle the intersection of political education and learning technologies from the engineering perspective focussing on design principles, implementation and evaluation. To scientifically guide this process, it answers the following research questions:

  1. How can educational role-plays be designed to align with contemporary political issues?
    • What are optimal parameters, prompts or other settings that produce useful scenarios?
    • How can a semi-automated generation pipeline be constrained to useful input for role-plays in an educational setting?
  2. How can computers assist in improving situatedness and immersion in political learning role-plays?
    • What are the effects of game elements on the game-specific experience metrics?
    • What are the results of the design decision in terms of technology acceptance and technical performance measurements?
  3. What is the effect on learning outcomes given the complex interaction of method, situation, learning group and technology?
    • How can deliberation skills be measured?
    • What is the impact of an analytics feedback loop on deliberative quality?
    • How can discussion analytics be implemented as a subset of learning analytics to improve educational technologies?

To handle these multiple challenges, the group will consist of three PhD students in addition to the group leader who are going to focus on the three parts: the Scenario Creation (SC), the Role-Playing Game Environment (RPGE) and the Deliberation Analytics (DA).

This keeps everyone connected and focused on the overarching objectives, fostering ongoing cohesion. The following describes these modules and the corresponding PhD projects in detail.

A) Scenario Creation (SC)

The planned simulation environment for political learning is composed of three primary components, each interconnected to create a cohesive system. The first component, the Scenario Creation (SC), supports generating [@davis_prompt_2024] and customizing the scenarios that drive the simulations. It leverages artificial intelligence to produce realistic scenarios [@vermeiren_assessing_2022], such as a European Parliament debate on a current issue, ensuring relevance and depth. Additionally, constraint programming [@gebser2011complex] is applied to tailor these scenarios according to specified parameters, including among others time limitations, learner group size, and the intended scope of knowledge [@finster2024;@rudian2023].

Being able to generate scenarios that apply to current events allows teachers to use the role-play method more regularly and with less work. Ideally, they would resemble the existing material [@dechant2017planspieldatenbank; @rappenglueck2017praxis] so that it can be used out-of-the-box. It also provides the basis for testing differences in the serious game setup with a larger number of game-runs.

In recent years, large language models like the Generative Pre-trained Transformer (GPT) have revolutionized natural language processing (NLP), particularly in their ability to generate coherent text, answer questions, and perform a wide array of language-related tasks with remarkable accuracy. These advancements stem largely from the adoption of transformer architectures [@devlin2018bert;@tay2022efficient], which have significantly enhanced the capability of models to manage long-range dependencies within a text. The transformer model, powered by the self-attention mechanism [@vaswani2017attention], excels at discerning the relevance of different text segments, regardless of their position within the input, allowing for a deep tracking of word relationships and the generation of complex word sequences such as role-playing scenarios.

The SC is going to be based on the transformer architecture but will need modification to include the game aspects in the output. The German database of educational political simulations and similar resources can be used [@dechant2017planspieldatenbank; @rappenglueck2017praxis] as training input. This part of the group will focus on finding optimal parameters, prompts or other settings that produce useful scenarios for role plays. Another challenge is going to be constraining the output to be useful and to cause little work for the teachers, so that they can focus their energy on the educational guidance. This is an effort better spent than working out the details of the roles. Although this seems difficult, there are certain properties of the use case that make it likely that this can be achieved given the current state of artificial intelligence [@SALEHIN202452].

Transformer-based text generation has been reported to produce hallucinations that don’t have any grounding in facts [@hallucination_2023]. But Simulations don’t need high realism, so web interfaces, desktop apps, augmented environments, or fully immersive virtual realities can be selected based on the use case [@axel_2021]. Less realism may well make it easier to take on the role for the students and enter the flow-experience [@zoerner_flow_2024]. It can also help to leave strong beliefs behind and be more open for the discussion. It is the teacher’s task in any case to transfer the learnings from the fictional setting to the presence.

The validation of the results could be in terms of usefulness, but the main evaluation will focus on algorithmic performance. For this reason, methodology from the NLP community will be prioritized. The same holds true for the constraining algorithms. For the above-shown reasons, the PhD student is going to need a strong background in computer science, particularly in the fields of NLP, Machine Learning or Logic Programming.

To ensure the best possible results, the PhD student benefits from strong connections at the University of Potsdam, which specializes in the research and teaching of constraint programming. Additionally, the group leader has expertise in this area, having previously taught the subject, and can contribute valuable insights and guidance.

B) Role-Playing Game Environment (RPGE)

The second component, the Role-Playing Game Environment (PPGE), provides the structural framework for the simulation. This modularized framework guides users through the simulation process, offering three distinct settings: institutionalized environments like Model United Nations [@duchatelet_explaining_2021], deliberation-focused discussions [@gold2017visual], and rational choice-oriented decision-making scenarios [@chen_otreeopen-source_2016]. This environment interfaces seamlessly with the Scenario Creation, allowing scenarios to be easily loaded into the system. To ensure flexibility and scalability, service-oriented principles [@Benatallah2008] are employed, enabling the decoupling of various elements. The environment is also going to support a range of hardware, from mobile devices to more complex setups like AR and VR [@Zender_Buchner_Schäfer_Wiesche_Kelly_Tüshaus_2022], creating a pervasive [@Zender2013] and immersive [@Mulders2023b] learning experience.

Role-plays are a standard method in education in areas where perspective-change is needed and rational judgment of social aspects is relevant. In the political education classroom, it is a typical approach to let students reenact two sides of a conflict such as pro and cons of a war decision or a presidential debate. This can also be much more elaborate up to simulations that can take a week.

One of the challenges is to support different levels on the continuum of role-plays and simulations. [@petrik2017alltagswelt] argues that the German word “Planspiele” (translates to ‘planned games’, the author) focuses more on conflict than on recipient-orientation which means that the planned institutional games are much more abstract and professionalized than the emotionality of acting in a role-playing exercise. In this case, the main conceptual challenge is to model these kinds of role-play variants.

This component is going to use concepts from serious game engineering [@hou_systematic_2023;@carlier_software_2023] and existing simulation software [@chen_otreeopen-source_2016]. The resulting software framework should support the various inputs and configurations the scenario creation provides. It is also necessary to generalize the game aspects to allow for different human-computer interactions, because “the use of simulations through VR or AR technologies […] allows conflicts experienced by learners to be authentically represented. As a result, data collection can be conducted in a laboratory setting while maintaining high ecological validity” [@HUANG2021104100] (translated using AI). Instead of overloading the software with all kind of implementations, it only needs to provide an interface for different scenario adapters. In one case, this could be a simple web interface, in another, an augmented reality setting could be supported where students simulate something more interactive like a United Nations conference. This approach keeps the workload for the software implementation manageable.

In this area there is significant existing work done at the computer science faculty that can be relied on: the group leader wrote his dissertation on a similar complex system. Besides, the hosting chair Ulrike Lucke teaches complex architectures and mentors a number of PhD students (i.e., Jan Bernoth and Axel Wiepke) that are happy to support the PhD candidate in terms of practical expertise and software development.

The PhD student is going to implement only one or two game types as a proof of concept for the framework that showcases the modularity and extendability. For this, the student needs a background in computer science with a focus on software engineering and experience with educational technologies. A strong interest in game development is recommended, too. In terms of input, one scenario from the role-play database should be implemented as a minimal working example of the software architecture to avoid blocking dependencies from the first component.

Apart from the common review paper (RPGE-1), the PhD student will start with the requirements engineering for the environment (RPGE-2). This will encompass software modeling, game design and some aspects of instructional design. The next deliverable is going to be a Minimal Example Prototype (RPGE-3) followed by a proof-of-concept implementation of a complex adapter (RPGE-4) such as a mobile interface. These software milestones will be accompanied by user tests regarding design choices and technology acceptance. Finally, the SC and DA components will be integrated (RPGE-5) and the dissertation written (RPGE-6).

C) Deliberation Analytics (DA)

The third component, Deliberation Analytics (DA), offers an automated framework that provides valuable insights into the discussions taking place during the simulation. This framework is designed to be transferable to other use cases, such as the National Educational Platform [@gleiss2023governance; @bustorff2023], where it can enhance or moderate online forums. Speech-to-text technology is integrated, interfacing with mobile devices to access and display real-time analytics [@gold2017visual]. Using natural language processing (NLP) techniques, this component assesses the quality of discussions by analyzing interaction patterns [@dehne2023consistent], identifying key arguments through argument mining [@lawrence2017using], and measuring the fairness of exchanges, including time distribution and the presence of uncivil speech [@papacharissi2004democracy;@friess2021collective].

This design not only supports political education by making role-plays more accessible. It creates a laboratory for teaching and studying deliberation, too. This could then be used in various fields such as ethical AI, environmental studies and many other fields where a healthy discussion culture and student engagement is required. Moreover, it is situated at the intersection of the three major fields of expertise of the applicant (computer science, education and political science) and thus would further qualify him for an academic career in computer-assisted (political) education and deliberation research.

Deliberation means a sensible, rational discussion where all the stakeholders come to a consensus that can be generally supported. It is a normative concept in political theory and can be traced back to Habermas’ discourse ethics [@habermas2009diskursethik] and Rawls’ “A Theory of Justice” [@rawls1975theorie]. It is a way of looking at discussions and assessing their quality [@steenbergen2003measuring] in terms of openness, tolerance and inclusiveness. The ability to participate in a productive discussion, use arguments, take on perspectives and moderate uncivil participants are important cornerstones of political education and beyond. To assess the result of role-plays, one way is to use the deliberative quality of the group discussion during the simulation as a proxy.

This part of the research group tackles the question of how deliberation skills are measured, how to best integrate these insights into the RPGE, and how this ties into the existing research in the area of learning analytics. This part is an important addition to the research group for the following reasons: a healthy discussion is not only relevant for a political education classroom. It should be desirable in all kinds of learning environments wherever group collaboration leads to dissemination and deliberation. Most significantly, it connects the research group to the strong trend of social media analytics in computational social science.

One of the technical tasks of this part of the group is to integrate the collection of speech data into the RPGE. Having access to an audio lifestream, speech-to-text technology is going to be used to have the discussion data available in the same format as discussion transcripts from social media. This is useful to reuse existing research and tooling [@dehne_dtrees_23;@duthie_classifying_2018;@gold_visual_2017]. In fact, the audio stream could also be directly piped into multimodal transformer models [@xu2023multimodallearningtransformerssurvey] if a more machine learning oriented approach yielded better results. The outcome of the analytics can be forwarded to the teacher or strategically presented within the RPGE right away.

Text data collected in the National Educational Platform [@gleiss2023governance; @bustorff2023] can be used as a provisional input to avoid a blocking dependency to an up-and-running RPGE.

The strong ties of the applicant to GESIS, Leibnitz Institute for Social Sciences, provide the necessary links and access to training in the area of computational social sciences. Having spent three years as a PostDoc in the field, the group leader is well-connected and can support the PhD student with necessary coaching of statistical and technical skills. As part of the newly formed German data science network (INFORMATIK Festival 2024) the group leader can also establish contact points at other institutions researching similar subjects.

The PhD student working on the DA part of the group needs a background in NLP, learning analytics or pedagogical psychology. A candidate from the field of computational social science might fit if the candidate can demonstrate strong computational skills.

D) Group Lead (GL): Teacher Facilitation and Learning Analytics

Evaluating the effect of complex pedagogical interventions is challenging. The fourth role in the group is going to focus on integrating different quality markers into one comprehensive framework. These include the technical performance markers included in the SC, the RPGE and the DA. But this also includes the effect on the teacher in terms of facilitation and the outcome for the learners in terms of personal development. [@DehneWL17] argue that the interaction of technology and education has been biased by the idea that the impact of technology always needs be evaluated in terms of learning outcomes. They argue that in some cases, the technology enhancement allows for types of learning experiences that could not be achieved otherwise. Moreover, in other cases, the main advantage of the technology is to facilitate teaching and reduce the workload in a compressed academic system.

The project aims at recruiting a panel of experienced teachers and students enrolled in teacher education programs. They will participate in the iterative evaluation of the technology prototype, following the principles of Design-Based Research (DBR) [@Reinmann.Januar2015]. This panel will not only assess the usability and effectiveness of the technology in each development cycle but will also serve as a benchmark to compare the difficulty of using the same methods without the aid of the technology. By establishing this gold standard, the project will provide a comprehensive basis for judging the extent to which the software facilitates the educational processes it is designed to support. Given that teachers are typically a challenging group to recruit for research studies due to workload constraints, this process will be coordinated with Lernen:Digital and the Potsdamer Zentrum für Lehrerbildung und Bildungsforschung (ZeLB). In addition to the trained teachers, teacher candidates and students in education programs will be approached via the established network within their respective institutions.

To address the impact on learners, existing research on learning analytics and learning outcomes from role-plays in political education is going to be combined into an analytical framework. If possible, a double-blind study will be conducted at the end of the four-year span. In this study, the control group is going to take part in a set of classical lessons, whereas the test group executes the role-play in the constructed environment. The group lead heads the experimental setup and prepares the surveys and accompanying tests. He supervises the software development, too.

The overarching goal for the group lead is to develop a methodology of supporting political education with computer-technology. This encompasses the use of …

  • … AI to generate learning content
  • … simulations to create an immersive and situated learning situation
  • … natural language processing to improve develop learning analytics for political learning

The developed prototype addresses the relevant forward-looking research areas, allowing the conceptual research to be empirically validated. This ensures that theoretical assumptions and hypotheses can be supported through practical application and empirical data.

Besides the common review paper (GL-1), the group lead will begin with networking for sample recruitment to support empirical studies (GL-2). The development of a sample recruitment strategy and a concept paper will follow (GL-3), ensuring a strong foundation for later work. The development of the learning environment will be continously orchestrated (GL-4), with a focus on integrating various components of the system (i.e., SC-3 and RPGE-3) in an agile development cycle and adding hooks that provide data for the analytics. This is followed by two studies: the first examining the facilitation effects of the RPGE (GL-5), and the second focusing on the learning outcomes derived from the RPGE (GL-6). With this, the group lead lays valuable ground for further project proposals on fundamental as well as applied research in the fields of learning technologies, deliberation analytics and political education.

Additionally, the New School of Digital Studies at the European University Viadrina in Frankfurt will serve as a key cooperation partner. Outside of Brandenburg Prof. David Plecher, leader of the augmented reality lab of the Technical University Munich (TUM), has confirmed collaborating and providing guidance with the process of developing an adapter for the RPGE based on augmented reality technologies.

The applicant brings an established network from the University of Göttingen, Humboldt University, and GESIS, the Leibniz Institute for the Social Sciences, to the University of Potsdam. While maintaining a strong connection with the University of Potsdam — having earned three of his four degrees there — the applicant has broadened his expertise and emancipated his personal focus to the field of computational social science. With this group proposal, he aims to combine both his previous and newly acquired knowledge, resulting in a rich methodological approach and promising, cutting-edge research.

The University of Potsdam has an international reputation for leading computer science research. It offers a valuable environment for the junior research group, for example, through the offerings of the Potsdam Graduate School for coaching and further training, as well as the research support services of the computing center. As both the leader of the competence network Lernen:Digital and the lead developer of the prototype for the National Education Platform, the University of Potsdam is uniquely positioned for research in media-supported education. The interdisciplinary research group would form a lasting link between the political education field and educational technologies, showcasing the innovative potential of the region Berlin-Brandenburg.
